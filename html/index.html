The full story...

Business Logic

I will try not to bore you too much with the details, but I currently work as a legal writer for an immigration law firm that helps scientists gain permanent visas to the United States. One thing we have to do is get information about every journal where they have published articles, how often they have been cited, etc. We would put this together for each application, but the information was never organized or stored anywhere, so each time we would start fresh. I found myself looking up the same information over and over again, and it frustrated me to think that someone across the room might be looking up the same journals and we had no way of sharing our work. Everyone knew the system was tiresome and inefficient, but no one had a solution.

Crawlers

After learning a little programming, I realized I could use those skills to make a better system. I began by writing a web crawler that used regular expressions to pull all the relevant information from a client's Google Scholar profile. Next, I built a second web crawler that pulled the top 10 journals from each Google Scholar category (sorry, Google) and stored that information in a Mongo database. Then, I wrote a script to parse a spreadsheet from Thomson Reuters showing the "Impact Factor" of every journal, and combined that with the Google Scholar rankings in the database. I also wrote code to determine if each article qualified as "highly cited" according to charts released by Thomson Reuters that rank by field of study, year of publication, and number of citations.


User Interface

In a perfect world, this all would have been very simple, but I found that there were so many inconsistencies in the data that the majority of the work I put into the app was focused on creating the interface that allows users to gather, edit and modify the data as needed. For instance, clients often list journals by abbreviation (or just plain spell them wrong), and Google Scholar and Thomson Reuters may differ in how they name the same journal. Users therefore need to be able to monitor for such inconsistencies and make changes as needed. At the same time, the whole purpose of the application is to save time, so I had to make the process as seamless as possible. While I know the user interface is not very flashy, it is intuitive, efficient and very effective at what it does. I think building an intuitive user interface was my favorite part of the project and I would love to do more work like this in the future.

Node voodoo magic

Another challenge I had to overcome is that we have to provide "evidence" of all information submitted to the immigration office. Thus, After obtaining the list of journal rankings, the user clicks a button to download a zip file of PDFs of each webpage where I pulled information. A lot of the heavy lifting is done by npm packages. I use a crawler (tinyreq) to grab the html source of each page; this is then fed to a phantom.js wrapper (html-to-pdf) that (you guessed it) converts the html to a pdf. These are then complied into a zip using jszip, and served to the user out of a temporary folder.

Maintenance

Finally, I had to build a "back-end" for the application where users could update and maintain the database, as journal rankings and citation percentiles are constantly being updated. This way, the application can remain in use in perpetuum even after I leave the company.

Benefits for the company

Using my app to gather this information takes between 5-10 minutes from start to finish. Before, it took half an hour minimum, and up to an hour or more for especially prolific clients. The company is still growing, but already with more than 50 employees using the app, I am probably saving the company close to 40 hours a week. It's like I've given them a full-time employee who works for free. I can't help but think that this is really awesome.

And for me

Personally, even though the application might not mean much to someone outside of the company, I am very proud of the work I put into it. I identified a problem, came up with a solution, wrote all the code, pitched the app to the company, oversaw testing-- everything. When I started, I knew almost nothing about back-end programing, and asynchronous code was a total mystery to me. I was learning everything for the first time, doing whatever needed to be done. Moreover, I was doing it all on my own initiative, on my own time, with no assurance that it would ever be used by anyone else. So, working on this project certainly helped me grow as a coder, but more importantly, it helped me grow as a developer-- as someone who can work through difficulties to deliver solutions for real-life problems. I am thrilled to have built something that people are using every day: something that improves productivity and saves employees from mindless toil. I can't wait to see what I can do while working as part of a team.

The above is a side-project I have been working on when I want a "break" from my other coding projects. It is in perpetual development, and while technically playable, I make no promises about it being fun.

About Me

I came into web development via an unusual route. I earned a BA in English in 2007, then graduated with a MFA in poetry from the University of Pittsburgh in 2009. A while later, I moved to South Korea, where I taught ESL for two years. I returned to the US in 2013, bought a house in Pittsburgh, got married, did some traveling, and spent a lot of time meditating. I began working as a professional writer for an immigration law firm in 2015, and began my journey as a developer the following year.

Although I had enjoyed teaching, it did not satisfy the creative impulse that had driven me to pursue a degree in English. While my job paid well enough, I was doing the same thing every week and wasn't learning or growing. When I heard a co-worker mention he was learning some computer programming, I decided to try it out myself. I was an easy convert, and it wasn't long until my nights and weekends were totally booked with projects and coursework from an assortment of online resources. As I developed more competence, I realized that coding could be the creative outlet I had been craving. Earlier this year, I finished what felt like my first "real" project, which is now being used to boost productivity at the company where I work. Seeing people using the application I built is such a thrill, and I look forward to see where this career will take me next.
